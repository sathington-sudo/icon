Hamilton Tech Hero Standing as ‘First Line of Defense’ Against Devastating Senior Scams

{/* Byline & Date info */}
By Special Correspondent | Hamilton Community Report
{/* Article Contents - Changed fully to highly-readable font-sans */}

HAMILTON, ON — For Chris Seymour, owner of Icon Computers at 90 Centennial Parkway North, fixing computers is only half the job. The other half has become a deeply personal mission: protecting Hamilton’s senior citizens from sophisticated cybercriminals looking to drain their life savings.

Seymour’s crusade began shortly after taking ownership of the shop in 2023, when a local elderly woman walked into his store, visibly shaken. She had fallen victim to a high-tech scam that cost her $12,000—money she will never get back.

{/* Reassuring Pull-Quote - Clean, bold modern layout, no italics/cursive elements */}

"Seeing her get scammed like that, so scared and devastated, broke my heart. Right then and there, I promised myself I would do everything in my power to make sure another senior in our community never has to go through that nightmare again."

— Chris Seymour, Owner of Icon Computers

Since 2023, it is estimated that Seymour protects local seniors from losing a staggering $250,000 every single year. Over his three years running the shop, that adds up to nearly three-quarters of a million dollars kept right where it belongs—in the pockets of our community's seniors, rather than the hands of international fraudsters.

How the Scammers Strike

According to Seymour, tech scams targeting older adults have become increasingly frequent and aggressive. They often start with a simple mistake, like accidentally clicking a link online. Suddenly, a chaotic pop-up flashes on the screen with loud warnings, falsely claiming the computer is locked or deeply infected with a virus.

From there, the trap is sprung. Victims are instructed to call a 1-800 number, where a convincing voice on the other end claims to be a certified technician from Microsoft or Apple.

"Quite often, they will trick the senior into downloading remote access software like AnyDesk or TeamViewer," Seymour explains. "Once they connect, the scammers have total control over the screen."

In some of the worst cases, scammers will fake a "Trojan horse" virus alert and claim the user's bank requires the entire computer to be wiped clean. They then demand heavy immediate credit card charges—or worse, manipulate the victim into transferring thousands of dollars under the guise of "fixing" the problem.

The Ultimate Red Flag: Gift Cards and Bitcoin

Seymour notes that as awareness grows, scammers are shifting their tactics to bypass traditional bank security. They do this by forcing victims to use payment methods that cannot be reversed.

{/* RED DANGER WARNING BOX */}

Guaranteed Scam Warning

"If someone on the phone tells you to go out and buy retail gift cards—like Apple, Google Play, or Amazon—or tells you to drive to a local store to put cash into a Bitcoin ATM machine, it is a 100 percent guaranteed scam," Seymour warns emphatically.

No legitimate tech support company, bank, or government agency will ever ask you to pay them in gift cards or cryptocurrency. Never, ever do it.

An Education in Safety

Instead of just wiping hard drives and sending clients on their way, Seymour takes a completely different approach. He donates his own time to sit down with seniors, patiently pulling back the curtain on how these digital traps operate.

"I hate seeing people get scared," Seymour says. "The scammers rely on panic. If we can replace that fear with education, the scammers lose all their power."

His primary rule for anyone facing a sudden, alarming computer issue is simple: hang up immediately and turn off the computer. Microsoft and major banks will never call out of the blue demanding remote access, cryptocurrency, or immediate gift card payments.

A Free Line of Defense

Seymour wants local residents to know they don't have to navigate the confusing digital world alone, and they certainly don't need to feel embarrassed if they get tricked. He encourages anyone in doubt to call or visit his shop before giving away a single dollar.

"If you see a scary pop-up, or if you have a bad feeling that someone is trying to scam you, don't pay them—call me first," says Seymour. "I will always take the time to look at your machine, verify if it's safe, and teach you what to look out for next time. We are here to help."
